\section{Introduction}

Simultaneous speech translation (SST) generates target-language text incrementally from a partial source speech stream.  This setting is essential for real-time lectures, conferences, and multilingual classrooms, but it is also unusually brittle: the system must act before hearing the full utterance, and emitted translations are difficult to revise without disrupting the user experience.

Most research settings treat the current speech stream as the primary input, optionally augmented with a narrow terminology glossary.  In real deployments, however, interpreters and audiences often have access to much richer context: presentation slides, a talk abstract, speaker information, prior lectures, paper PDFs, course notes, and a domain glossary.  Slides are particularly natural for academic talks and professional courses because they contain terminology, equations, names, and visual concepts that are often under-specified in the audio signal.

We argue that slide-aware contextual SST is not simply a matter of adding more context to the prompt.  Slides are asynchronous and noisy.  Speakers may skip a slide, return to a previous slide, summarize future content, or discuss off-slide details.  Background documents can also be too broad, and glossary entries can conflict with the current utterance.  Under a streaming latency budget, irrelevant context can cause the model to translate a slide term before the speaker says it, or to select the wrong English rendering for a Chinese homophone.

This paper studies the following question: \emph{When do slides and deployment context help SST, and when do they hurt?}  We focus on Chinese-to-English lecture translation, where homophones and near-homophones make the value of slide context especially clear.  For example, the source pronunciation \emph{xiancheng} may correspond to ``thread'' or ``ready-made''; a current slide containing ``thread scheduling'' should help, but a stale slide about another topic should not.

We make three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item We define \taskname, a slide-aware contextual SST task where a streaming translator can use audio, current/past slide evidence, glossary entries, and background documents under latency constraints.
    \item We construct \datasetname, a minimal Chinese lecture challenge set targeting homophones, terminology, entity names, and slide-speech mismatch.  The benchmark includes adversarial context variants so that context overuse can be measured, not merely translation quality.
    \item We propose \policyname, a lightweight evidence-aware context manager that retrieves compact evidence packets and applies a conservative use/ignore/delay policy.  Our experiments compare no-context, glossary-only, slide-only, naive all-context, and policy-based systems.
\end{enumerate}
